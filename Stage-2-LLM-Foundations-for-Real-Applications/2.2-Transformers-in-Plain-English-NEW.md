## Core Mental Model

### The Parallel Context Processing Frame

When you read a sentence, you process it left to right, one word at a time, building meaning incrementally. If a word is ambiguous, you use context you've already read to resolve it.

A transformer processes context differently: **it looks at the entire context at once, and for every token, it asks — given everything else here, what does this specific token mean right now?**

This is not sequential. It is parallel. Every token simultaneously attends to every other token. The result is a context-sensitive representation of each token — not its dictionary definition, but its meaning in this specific context, shaped by everything around it.

This frame answers two questions that confuse engineers constantly:

**Why does prompt structure matter?** When you move an instruction from the top of a prompt to the middle of a long context, you change which tokens surround it and how attention distributes over the full sequence. The model does not read your prompt top to bottom like a person — it processes it as a web of relationships between tokens. Structure changes those relationships.

**Why do long prompts sometimes perform worse than short ones?** Attention distributes across all tokens. More tokens means each one gets proportionally less attention on average. If your relevant instruction is surrounded by thousands of tokens of less relevant text, it competes with that text for attention. Dense, relevant context outperforms large, diluted context.

These are not intuitions — they are direct consequences of the mechanism. Every concept in this lesson is one part of that mechanism explained plainly.

## Main Concepts + Production Reality

### Concept 1 — Embeddings: Meaning as Position in Space

The model cannot process raw text characters. Before anything else happens, each token (→ 2.1) is converted into an **embedding** — a vector of numbers that encodes the token's meaning as a position in a very high-dimensional space.

The intuition: imagine placing words on a map where position represents meaning, not alphabetical order. "Cat" and "kitten" are close together. "Cat" and "dog" are nearby. "Cat" and "automobile" are far apart. "Cat" and "feline" are almost at the same location. This is the structure of embedding space — meaning encoded as geometry.

Real embedding spaces have hundreds or thousands of dimensions rather than two, which allows them to encode much richer relationships. One of the most cited observations from early embedding research:

```
king − man + woman ≈ queen
Paris − France + Germany ≈ Berlin
walked − walk + run ≈ ran
```

Relationships between concepts are encoded as directions and distances in this space. The model did not learn these rules explicitly — it learned them implicitly by observing which tokens appear in similar contexts across billions of sentences during pretraining.

**Engineering consequences:**

*Paraphrase handling is free.* "Summarize this ticket," "give me a summary of this ticket," and "what's the gist of this ticket" land in similar regions of embedding space. The model treats them similarly without you having to account for every phrasing variant. Standard English vocabulary: expect good handling.

*Domain-specific and internal terminology is not free.* Internal product codes, proprietary abbreviations, company-specific jargon, and terms that were rare in pretraining data may have sparse or inaccurate embeddings. The model places them somewhere in the space based on limited context — which may be wrong for your use case. If the model consistently mishandles a specific term, the embedding is likely the root cause. Options: define the term explicitly in the prompt, or fine-tune (→ Stage 4).

*Why this matters when you add a new endpoint to the copilot:* Before assuming the model will handle your domain's vocabulary correctly, test the specific terms your tickets use. Ticket IDs, internal system names, product version codes — test them. Don't assume.

### Concept 2 — Attention: Context-Sensitive Meaning

Once each token has its initial embedding, the transformer applies **attention** — a mechanism that refines each token's representation by computing which other tokens in the context are most relevant to understanding it right now.

The classic example: the word "bank" in two sentences.

```
"I walked along the river bank at sunset."
"My bank account was charged twice this month."
```

Both sentences start with "bank" having the same initial embedding. After attention, the representations diverge. In the first sentence, attention finds strong relevance between "bank" and "river," "walked," "along" — pulling the representation toward geography. In the second, attention finds strong relevance between "bank" and "account," "charged" — pulling it toward finance. Same token, different meaning, because attention used the surrounding context to refine the representation.

This is contextual understanding — and it happens for every token simultaneously across the full context.

**What attention means for prompt engineering — the lost-in-the-middle problem:**

Attention is not uniformly distributed. Research and production experience consistently show that across long contexts, models attend more strongly to tokens near the beginning and end of the context than to tokens in the middle. This is not a quirk of a specific model — it is observed across transformer architectures generally.

```
What this looks like in a real system:

  [System prompt — 300 tokens — key rule: "Always return valid JSON only"]
  [Policy block — 2,400 tokens — buried rule at ~token 1,500: "Never mention competitor X"]
  [User ticket — 300 tokens]

  Observed behavior:
  - "Return valid JSON" → followed reliably (system prompt, beginning of context)
  - "Never mention competitor X" → followed inconsistently (middle of a long block)

  The model is not broken. It is not randomly ignoring rules.
  The rule at position 1,500 is receiving less attention weight
  than the rule at position 0–300. Same effect as if it were less emphasized.
```

**What to do about it — in order of priority:**

1. Put the most critical behavioral rules in the system prompt, at the top. Not in a long policy block buried further down.
2. Within any long context block, lead with the most important content.
3. For prompts over roughly 2,000 tokens, consider repeating critical constraints at the end of the context.
4. Do not assume that including a rule in the prompt guarantees it will be followed — at high context lengths, verify compliance empirically.

This is one of the most directly actionable insights from transformer architecture. It explains a class of failures that look like "the model isn't following my instructions" but are actually "the model's attention isn't reaching those instructions reliably." The fix is structural, not a model upgrade.

### Concept 3 — Layers: Stacked Refinement

Attention does not happen once. The transformer applies it repeatedly through a stack of layers — typically 32 to 96 in production-scale models. Each layer takes the previous layer's output and applies attention plus a transformation, producing a richer representation.

The representations evolve through this stack at increasing levels of abstraction:

```
Input token embeddings (raw)
        ↓
Early layers (1–6 approx.)
  Local patterns: grammar, punctuation roles, adjacent word relationships
  "this token is a verb," "these tokens form a noun phrase"
        ↓
Middle layers (7–20 approx.)
  Phrase-level semantics: what groups of words mean together
  Entity recognition, coreference ("it" and "they" refer to what)
  Topic signals begin to emerge
        ↓
Later layers (21–32+ approx.)
  Discourse-level: what is the full context about
  Long-range dependencies: relationships between distant tokens
  Abstract reasoning patterns that span the full context
        ↓
Final representation → used for next-token prediction (→ 2.1)
```

By the final layer, each token's representation encodes not just its own meaning but its relationship to everything else in the context at multiple levels of abstraction. This is why the model can handle complex language tasks — not because it stores answers, but because it builds rich multi-layer representations of each input.

**Engineering consequences:**

*More layers means the model can capture longer-range relationships.* A question about the "subject" in a sentence that is 200 tokens long requires attention relationships to span 200 tokens. Deeper models handle this better than shallower ones.

*Complex tasks need structured, relevant context — not just more context.* Adding content to a prompt gives the later layers more to process. But adding irrelevant content adds noise at every layer without adding useful signal. The quality of context matters more than its volume.

*Chain-of-thought prompting works through this mechanism.* When you ask the model to "think step by step," the intermediate reasoning tokens become part of the context and pass through subsequent attention layers. Each step becomes part of the representation that later tokens attend to. The model is not reasoning in a magical way — it is building representations that include prior reasoning as context, and those representations are richer than what a single-step prompt would produce. (→ practical patterns in 2.5)

*You do not need to know which layer does what.* This layer breakdown is the intuition. In production, the consequence is: deeper models with more layers generally handle more complex tasks better, but they cost more and run slower. Model selection (→ 2.12) involves these tradeoffs.

### Concept 4 — Context Window: The Architectural Capacity Limit

The attention mechanism requires every token to attend to every other token. Computational cost scales roughly with the square of the number of tokens — doubling the context length roughly quadruples the computation for attention. This is why context windows are limited — not by an arbitrary configuration choice, but by the architecture.

Modern models extend this through techniques like sparse attention and sliding window attention, but the fundamental constraint remains. Every model has a maximum context length that is an architectural property, not a tunable parameter.

**What this means for engineering:**

*The limit is in tokens, not words or pages.* 128,000 tokens is approximately 90,000–100,000 English words, or roughly 300 dense pages. But code, JSON, structured data, and many non-English languages tokenize less efficiently than English prose — a context fills faster than raw word count suggests. The exact mechanics of measuring this are in 2.3, where you build the token counting utility.

*Fitting in context ≠ attending to it equally.* This is the most important and most frequently misunderstood point about the context window. A 128k limit means the model *can* process 128k tokens in one call. It does not mean it will attend to all 128k tokens with equal reliability. The lost-in-the-middle effect applies regardless of context size — a larger window shifts where "the middle" is, it does not eliminate the phenomenon.

*The context window is a processing window, not a filing cabinet.*

```
COMMON MISTAKE — Context stuffing:

Team has 40 internal policy documents (~60,000 tokens total).
They concatenate all 40 into the system prompt.
Model answers common-topic questions well.
Model gives vague or wrong answers for niche topics buried in document 23.

Why it fails:
  The relevant content is spread across 60,000 tokens.
  Attention distributes across all of it.
  For niche questions, the relevant 500 tokens are surrounded by
  59,500 tokens of less relevant content.
  Signal-to-noise ratio is too low.

The fix:
  Retrieve only the relevant chunks at query time.
  That is what RAG does (→ Stage 3).
  A 2,000-token context of highly relevant chunks outperforms
  a 60,000-token context of everything.
```

*Context window is a product design constraint.* The features you can build depend on how much context they consume. Single-turn ticket classification uses ~1,500 tokens per call. Multi-turn conversation with full history grows with every exchange. Document Q&A over large corpora cannot fit in context at all. These are architectural decisions, made at design time.

**Context budget for the copilot — rough estimates:**

```
Endpoint       System prompt   Instructions   Content        Tools/history   Output    Total est.
/classify      ~500 tok        ~150 tok       ~300 tok       —               ~300 tok  ~1,250
/extract       ~500 tok        ~250 tok       ~400 tok       —               ~600 tok  ~1,750
/summarize     ~400 tok        ~150 tok       ~500 tok       —               ~1,200 tok ~2,250
/route         ~500 tok        ~250 tok       ~400 tok       ~800 tok        ~600 tok  ~2,550
/chat          ~500 tok        ~150 tok       variable       variable        ~800 tok  variable
```

These are rough estimates. In 2.3 you will measure them precisely using tiktoken and build the budget enforcement utility. The point right now: no single endpoint is anywhere near the 128k limit, but the budget must be tracked — unchecked growth in system prompts, history, or retrieved content will push you toward limits faster than expected.

## Key Takeaways

- **Context structure is not neutral.** The transformer processes your prompt as a web of attention relationships between tokens — not as a document read sequentially. Where you place things changes what attends to what, and therefore what the model generates.

- **Lost in the middle is real, architectural, and actionable.** Content in the middle of long contexts receives less attention weight. Place critical instructions at the start. For prompts over ~2,000 tokens, repeat key constraints at the end. Do not bury important rules in large blocks.

- **Dense relevant context beats large diluted context, always.** Adding tokens does not linearly improve quality. Every irrelevant token competes with relevant tokens for attention weight. Include only what the model needs for this specific task.

- **The context window is a processing limit, not storage.** Fitting content into context does not mean the model attends to it reliably. Plan context budgets explicitly — measure them in 2.3 and enforce them.

- **Paraphrase handling is free; internal jargon is not.** Standard vocabulary is well-embedded from pretraining. Domain-specific terminology, product codes, and internal naming conventions may be poorly embedded. Test them before assuming the model handles them correctly.

- **RAG is the architectural answer to context window limits.** Stuffing all available context into a prompt is not a scaling strategy — it is the problem RAG solves. Dense, retrieved, relevant context outperforms large, diluted, concatenated context at every scale. This is why Stage 3 exists.

## Optional Advanced Notes

**Multi-head attention:** The attention mechanism runs in parallel across multiple "heads" — each learning to attend to different types of relationships (one may learn syntactic relationships, another semantic, another long-range coreference). This is why transformers are expressive: they capture many kinds of token relationships simultaneously. The engineering consequence at this stage is fully covered by the concept above. Deeper understanding of heads is relevant for interpretability and fine-tuning (→ Stage 4).

**Positional encodings:** The attention mechanism has no inherent notion of token order — it computes relationships between all token pairs regardless of distance. Positional encodings are added to embeddings to inject position information, allowing the model to distinguish "bank near river" from "bank near account" based on position as well as content. Modern models use rotary positional encodings (RoPE) and similar techniques. The engineering implication (position matters) is fully covered. Mechanics belong in Stage 4.

**Why 128k and 200k context windows still have lost-in-the-middle behavior:** Larger context windows extend the architectural limit, but they do not change how attention distributes over long sequences. A 128k window means the "middle" is now around token 64,000 instead of token 4,000. The phenomenon applies at whatever scale the context is filled. Empirical testing on very long contexts consistently confirms this.
