## Core Mental Model

### The Pattern Completion Frame

Your smartphone keyboard predicts the next word as you type. Type "I'll be there in" and it suggests "five minutes" or "a bit." It has no idea where you are or what you mean — it recognizes that these words typically follow that phrase in human communication. The prediction is statistical, not semantic.

An LLM is this principle operating at a fundamentally different scale: trained on essentially all digitized human text — books, articles, code, forums, documentation, support tickets, scientific papers, legal contracts — and capable of predicting what token comes next across virtually every domain of human knowledge. The result is a model that can write an essay, generate a SQL query, classify a support ticket, or explain a concept with apparent competence and fluency.

The mechanism is the same: **it generates what is statistically likely to follow the preceding context.**

This is the most important engineering insight in this stage. Hold it clearly before reading anything else in this lesson, because every concept that follows is an explanation of either how that mechanism was built or what it implies for system design.

The frame explains four things engineers get wrong constantly:

**1. Why LLMs can seem authoritative.**
If the correct answer was well-represented in training data, the most probable continuation is often correct. The model learned from millions of instances of people explaining, answering, and demonstrating. When you ask it to explain recursion or write a regex or summarize a paragraph, you are asking it to continue a pattern it has seen countless times. It is good at this.

**2. Why LLMs can be confidently wrong.**
When the exact fact is niche, post-cutoff, or proprietary — your company's pricing, a client's account balance, a system behavior from last month — the model still generates a continuation. The probability distribution shifts toward "what would plausibly come next in a text about this topic" rather than "what is actually true." There is no internal gate that says "I am not confident about this." Confidence is a property of fluent generation, not a signal of accuracy. This is not a bug. It is the mechanism.

**3. Why prompts work.**
You are setting the context that shapes what continuation is probable. "You are a support routing assistant. The ticket says:" creates a completely different probability distribution than no context at all. Prompts are not commands you issue to a reasoning engine — they are context you provide to a pattern-completion engine. Understanding this changes how you design them. (→ 2.5)

**4. Why your system design must do what the model cannot.**
The model cannot verify claims, access real-time data, look up account information, enforce access controls, or know what happened after its training cutoff. These are not limitations to wait for a better model to fix — they are structural properties of the mechanism. They are engineering responsibilities.

Every concept in this lesson builds on this frame. Keep it active as you read.

## Main Concepts + Production Reality

### Concept 1 — Tokens: The Unit of Everything

The model does not read words or characters. It reads **tokens** — chunks of text produced by a tokenizer that splits input into pieces based on frequency patterns in the training data.

A token is typically 3–4 characters. Common English words like "cat," "the," and "run" are single tokens. Longer or rarer words split into multiple tokens: "unbelievable" might tokenize as `["un", "believ", "able"]`. Code, numbers, punctuation, and whitespace tokenize differently than prose.

What this means for the pattern-completion frame: the model is not predicting the next *word* — it is predicting the next *token*, one step at a time, based on all previous tokens in the context.

**The engineering consequence is not about linguistics — it is about cost, latency, and limits:**

- Every API pricing model charges per token (input + output)
- Latency scales with token count — more tokens in, more tokens out, longer it takes
- Context limits are in tokens: "128k context window" means 128,000 tokens, roughly 90,000–100,000 words
- When your prompt says "be concise," you are trying to reduce output tokens, which directly reduces cost and latency

Design your system in tokens, not in words, sentences, or pages. When you plan a prompt, think about its token cost. When you plan a context budget, measure in tokens and reserve explicitly for output. The practical mechanics of counting and managing token budgets are covered in 2.3, where you build the token utility module. For now, internalize the unit.

### Concept 2 — Next-Token Prediction: The Generation Mechanism

Given a sequence of tokens, the model predicts which token is most likely to come next. That token is appended to the sequence, and the model predicts the next one. This loop continues until the model produces a stop token or hits the max output length.

**Every response — summary, classification, code, answer — is the output of this loop.**

This means several things that matter for engineering:

**Output is a continuation of context.** The model does not generate a response "from scratch." It extends what came before. This is why the structure of your prompt — what you say first, what order you say it in, how you frame the task — directly influences what comes out. You are shaping the starting sequence that the prediction loop extends.

**Early tokens influence later tokens.** Because each prediction step uses all previous tokens as context, errors or framings established early in the generation propagate forward. A model that starts a response incorrectly often continues that direction. This is why few-shot examples in prompts work (→ 2.5) — correct early examples set the pattern the generation follows.

**The same input can produce different outputs.** Prediction at each step involves sampling from a probability distribution, not always taking the single most probable token. This means the model is non-deterministic by default. You control this behavior through temperature (→ 2.4). For classification tasks in the copilot, you want near-deterministic outputs. For drafting tasks, some variation is acceptable.

**The critical consequence of the mechanism:** the model produces what is *probable*, not what is *true*. These frequently align — and when they do, the output looks excellent. When they diverge, the output looks equally confident and is wrong. There is no internal signal that distinguishes these cases. This is the root cause of hallucination (→ 2.10 for full treatment, detection heuristics, and mitigation strategies).

### Concept 3 — Pretraining: Where Capability Comes From

Pretraining is the initial, large-scale training phase where the model learns by predicting tokens across an enormous corpus — web text, books, code, academic papers, documentation, forums. During this phase, the model learns:

- Language patterns: grammar, sentence structure, argument construction, discourse conventions
- Domain knowledge: what medical literature sounds like, how support tickets are typically written, how Python code is structured, how legal contracts are phrased
- World knowledge: factual associations well-represented in the training data
- Format conventions: JSON, markdown, code blocks, bullet lists, table structures

**What pretraining gives you:** a model that can generate fluent, contextually appropriate text across virtually any domain — without any domain-specific training. This is the source of almost all the model's apparent competence.

**What pretraining does NOT give you, and this is where engineers make expensive mistakes:**

| What engineers assume | What is actually true |
|---|---|
| The model knows our pricing | It knows what pricing text looks like in general |
| The model knows the current state | Its knowledge is frozen at the training cutoff |
| The model looked this up | It generated a continuation based on patterns |
| Authoritative tone = reliable fact | Tone is a generation property; accuracy is not |
| A newer model fixes the data problem | Newer pretraining still doesn't include your internal data |

The engineering implication for the copilot: when a feature requires the model to accurately report anything about your system state — ticket statuses, account data, policy values, inventory levels — pretraining cannot supply that. No prompt engineering, no model upgrade, and no amount of retry logic changes this. The data must be injected via retrieval, tool calls, or context. These patterns are built in 2.8 (tool calling) and Stage 3 (RAG). The capability boundary document you build in this lesson is the first articulation of this line.

### Concept 4 — Instruction Tuning: What Makes It an Assistant

A base pretrained model given the prompt "Classify this support ticket:" will produce a continuation — but it is just as likely to produce more ticket text, a comment thread, a classification rubric explanation, or a training dataset row. The model learned "what comes after text" broadly. It was not trained to answer questions or follow task instructions.

Instruction tuning is the subsequent training phase where the model learns task-following behavior. The training data consists of (instruction → good response) pairs, often with human preference labeling (RLHF — Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) to align outputs toward helpful, safe, cooperative behavior.

What instruction tuning changes:
- The model follows task-style prompts reliably: summarize, classify, extract, explain, draft, rewrite
- It responds in an assistant register: cooperative, structured, responsive to constraints you give it
- It handles multi-turn conversations: it understands that it is an assistant responding to a user, not just continuing text
- It acknowledges some limits: "I don't have access to your account information" is an instruction-tuned behavior, not a base model behavior

**What instruction tuning does NOT change — and this is the misconception that causes the most production incidents:**

Instruction tuning makes the model *cooperative*, not *correct*. These are different properties.

- A model that follows "return only valid JSON" is better at formatting, not better at facts
- A model that sounds polite and structured is not more reliable at specific claims
- A model that follows your task framing perfectly can still hallucinate the content within that framing
- Instruction tuning improved alignment with human preferences — it did not add a fact-verification mechanism

When the model says "Your refund will be processed in 3–5 business days" with perfect confidence and JSON structure, it is exhibiting excellent instruction-tuning behavior and potentially generating a completely invented fact. Both are true at the same time.

This is the capability boundary: the model is a highly cooperative language engine. It is not a data retrieval system. Designing against that boundary is what separates systems that work reliably in production from systems that work in demos.

## Tasks the LLM Handles Directly

These tasks require language transformation of *provided* content only.
The model is not asked to retrieve data, claim external facts, or take actions.

| Task | Input | Why it's safe |
|---|---|---|
| Classify ticket into category | Ticket text | Classification is based on the text provided; model doesn't need external data |
| Summarize ticket | Ticket text | Summarization is a language transformation of provided content |
| Draft suggested reply | Ticket text + context provided in prompt | Drafting from supplied context, not from claimed facts |
| Extract structured fields from ticket text | Ticket text | Extraction from provided source; grounding check added in 2.10 |

---

## Tasks That Require External Grounding

These tasks require data the LLM cannot have. The LLM must NOT answer 
from generation alone — it requires retrieval, tool calls, or explicit injection.

| Task | Why grounding is required | Grounding mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| Report current ticket status | Live database state; model has no access | Tool call → ticket_lookup (→ 2.8) |
| Answer questions about account billing | Private account data; not in training | Tool call or RAG (→ Stage 3) |
| Apply company-specific routing rules | Internal policy data; not public | Policy injection via prompt or RAG |
| Confirm whether a fix was deployed | Real-time system state | Tool call or retrieval |

---

## Tasks Outside LLM Scope Entirely

These tasks must not use an LLM as a component, even with grounding.

| Task | Why |
|---|---|
| Authenticate users | Authentication is a security-critical operation; LLM outputs are not deterministic |
| Execute billing changes | Irreversible action; requires deterministic logic with full audit trail |
| Compute exact SLA compliance | Arithmetic precision requirement; LLMs can make calculation errors |

---

## Decision Rule

Use the LLM directly for: language transformation of provided content.

Add retrieval, tool calls, or validation when:
- The answer requires data not in the prompt
- The answer must be factually exact
- The output triggers an irreversible downstream action

---

## Review Trigger

Update this document before adding any new endpoint to the copilot.

## Key Takeaways

- **The model generates probable continuations — not verified facts.** Fluency and accuracy are different properties produced by different aspects of training. When they look the same in the output, the mechanism is working correctly. When they diverge, the model has no way to signal it.

- **Design your system in tokens, not words.** Cost, latency, and context limits all operate in token space. Everything else — prompts, summaries, context budgets, output sizes — is measured in tokens in production.

- **Pretraining gives capability; it does not give access.** The model can generate excellent-sounding text about any domain. It cannot retrieve your company's data, know what happened after its cutoff, or look anything up. No model upgrade changes this — the architecture requires explicit data injection for grounding.

- **Instruction tuning makes the model cooperative, not correct.** A model that follows your task framing perfectly can still generate wrong content within that framing. These are independent properties. Never infer reliability from compliance.

- **The capability boundary is an architectural decision, not a limitation to work around.** Knowing what the model can do directly and what requires grounding is the first design decision in every LLM system. Make it explicit, in writing, before building.

- **Every production failure in an LLM system has a root cause in this lesson.** Wrong task assignment, missing retrieval, absent validation, hallucinated facts presented as real — all trace back to the gap between what the pattern-completion mechanism does and what the system was designed assuming it could do.

## Optional Advanced Notes

**Base models vs. instruction-tuned models in production:** Most production API access is to instruction-tuned models (GPT-4o, Claude Sonnet, Gemini Pro). Base models are available but rarely used directly in application code — they require explicit prompt formatting to produce usable outputs and are less predictable under varied inputs. Unless you are doing specific fine-tuning work, you will always be working with instruction-tuned models. Stage 4 covers fine-tuning, where the distinction matters in more depth.

**RLHF details:** Instruction tuning encompasses several techniques. RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) and its variants (RLAIF, DPO) are the methods used to align model outputs with human preferences. The mechanics matter for fine-tuning (Stage 4) but not for application engineering at this stage — knowing that instruction tuning converted a text predictor into an assistant is sufficient here.
